About This Visualization

Rationale for Design Decisions

The central question — where does carbon go, and what does it cost us? — motivated a three-panel timeseries layout that traces the carbon cycle from source to consequence. Atmospheric CO₂ mass and global temperature sit in the top row because their correlation is the most direct causal link in climate science. Ocean CO₂ uptake sits below as the primary absorber that buffers how much CO₂ remains in the atmosphere. A spatial dot-map of historical land carbon storage grounds the abstract timeseries in physical geography.

We chose line charts over bar charts or area charts because we are comparing continuous timeseries across scenarios. Color encodes scenario identity consistently across all three panels (gray = historical, orange = SSP2-4.5, red = SSP5-8.5), so the reader builds an immediate mental model that carries across every view.

The key interaction is linked brushing on the context strip: selecting a time range on the bottom overview zooms all three timeseries charts simultaneously. This lets users compare the scenarios at any temporal resolution — from the full 1850–2100 arc down to a specific decade. A crosshair tooltip on mousemove shows exact values for all active scenarios at the hovered year, fulfilling the details-on-demand requirement without cluttering the chart permanently. Scenario toggle buttons allow readers to isolate or compare specific pathways.

The spatial land carbon map uses a Natural Earth projection with a yellow-green sequential color scale, which perceptually encodes carbon density intuitively (darker = more carbon). Zoom and pan via d3.zoom() let users drill into specific regions; a tooltip on hover gives exact latitude, longitude, and carbon density.

Alternatives considered: a globe projection (harder to read precise locations), a choropleth by country (loses sub-national spatial variation), and a heatmap grid (looked too abstract). The dot-map approach preserves the actual CMIP6 grid resolution and makes sparse polar regions legible.

Development Process

Data was sourced from the CMIP6 cloud-hosted Zarr archive on Google Cloud Storage, accessed via gcsfs and xarray in Python. Variables extracted: co2mass and tas from GFDL-CM4; fgco2 from GFDL-ESM4 (esm-hist for the historical period, SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 for projections); and the cLand spatial mean from GFDL-ESM4 historical for the land carbon map. All timeseries were aggregated to annual global means and exported as CSV files for browser loading. Data was aggregated to annual global means and exported as CSV files for browser loading.

The front-end was built entirely in D3.js v7 with no other plotting libraries. Approximately 20 person-hours were spent on the project total: roughly 6 hours on data exploration and extraction (Python/xarray pipeline), 8 hours on the D3 visualization (linked brushing and multi-panel coordination took the most time), 3 hours on layout/styling, and 3 hours on write-up and deployment.

The most technically challenging aspect was coordinating the brush-zoom interaction across three independent SVGs sharing a single x-scale domain, while also keeping tooltip crosshairs synchronized across panels on mousemove.
